## The Story

The day started with the simple, chaotic joy of a water fight with friends. We were soaked, happy, and finally, ready to head home. I pulled out my phone, confident in my map app to guide us.

But as the sun began to set, the familiar roads were nowhere to be found. A journey that should have taken 30 minutes turned into a frustrating, 20km detour through unfamiliar streets. I was angry—at the map for being wrong, at the bad signal, at the world. It felt like everything was conspiring against me.

## The Real-world Lesson

That feeling of blaming the map is something I now recognize from my work. How many times have I been stuck on a bug and immediately thought, "This library is trash," or "This documentation is useless"? It's the same pattern: placing blame on external factors that are completely out of my control.

## Psychological Insight: The Courage to Be Disliked

This experience brought to mind a core concept from Alfred Adler's psychology, famously explored in the book *The Courage to Be Disliked*: the **"Separation of Tasks"** (課題の分離).

The principle is simple but profound: you must differentiate between *your* tasks and *other people's* tasks. The map being wrong was not my task to fix. The traffic was not my task. My task was to navigate safely home with the information I had. Wasting energy being angry at the map was a futile attempt to control something I couldn't.

## Redefining Maturity

I used to think maturity was about age or achieving certain milestones. Now, I see it as the ability to practice this Separation of Tasks in real-time. It's the quiet confidence to accept what you cannot change and pour all your energy into what you *can*. It’s understanding that complaining about a problem is not the same as solving your part of it.

## Separation of Tasks in Engineering

In engineering, this mindset is a superpower.

-   A third-party API is down? That's their task. My task is to implement a graceful fallback for my users and communicate the issue.
-   A bug exists in an open-source library? The maintainer's task is to eventually fix it. My task is to create a minimal reproducible example, submit a clear bug report, or find a temporary workaround.
-   A design specification is unclear? That's a shared problem. My task is not to make assumptions, but to ask for clarification.

This mindset doesn't make you passive. It makes you incredibly effective. You stop being a victim of circumstance and start being the driver of your own actions, focusing your finite energy on the things that truly move the needle.
